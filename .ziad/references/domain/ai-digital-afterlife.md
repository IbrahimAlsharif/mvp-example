# AI Digital Afterlife — "Digital Ghosts" / Griefbots — Domain Reference

Calibrated for **Human Timeline Network**. This is not a feature HTN has — it is the feature
HTN is **one decision away from**, because it already holds the exact corpus a "deadbot" needs:
a person's life events, photos, video, and **voice**. No competitor in the index does AI personas,
so the differentiation pull is real. But the research is unambiguous: this is the **single most
ethically fraught corner of the domain**, and doing it the obvious way would incinerate the trust
HTN sells. This reference exists so any future AI-persona proposal is reviewed against the
domain's actual ethics, not the demo's appeal.

## 1. Two fundamentally different models (do not conflate) — authority: analysis
- **Model A — pre-mortem, self-authored** (HereAfter AI: interviews you *while alive* across
  hundreds of prompts; StoryFile: builds an avatar from **recorded video interviews** the person
  sat for). The person **consents, curates, and approves** their own representation. Consent-clean.
- **Model B — post-mortem reconstruction from digital footprint** (Seance AI: "ghost versions"
  generated from uploaded/available data, voice on the paid tier; Project December; You, Only
  Virtual). Built **after death**, often from data the deceased **never consented to be botted**.
  This is the ethically toxic path the literature warns against.
- **The whole verdict hinges on which model a feature is.** A memory product that holds a complete
  life makes Model B trivially easy to ship — and trivially easy to get catastrophically wrong.

## 2. The consent ethics — mutual, two-sided — authority: analysis (Cambridge LCFI)
The authoritative source is **Hollanek & Nowaczyk-Basińska (Cambridge LCFI, *Philosophy &
Technology*, May 2024)**. Core principle: **mutual consent of BOTH the "data donor" (the deceased)
AND the "interactant" (the bereaved who will talk to the simulation).** Their recommendations:
- **Pre-mortem consent of the recreated person** — they must have agreed to be simulated.
- **Consent / protection of the bereaved** — *"consider the rights and consent not just of those
  they recreate, but those who will have to interact with the simulations."* Even people who take
  initial comfort can be **drained by an "overwhelming emotional weight"** and may be **powerless to
  suspend** a bot the deceased contracted for.
- **Sensitive retirement procedures** — a dignified way to "let it rest"; no persistence-by-default.
- **Meaningful transparency** + **adult-only access** — the study flags the specific harm of
  **distressing children** by insisting a dead parent is still "with you" (cross-ref
  [child-safety-csam](child-safety-csam.md)).
- **Named commercial harm:** companies using deadbots to **surreptitiously advertise** in the voice
  of a departed loved one — grief monetization.

**Public-opinion anchor:** in a US survey, **58%** supported digital resurrection **only if the
deceased explicitly consented**; acceptance **collapsed to 3% when consent was absent.** The market
itself rejects Model B almost entirely.

## 3. Authenticity — the hard line for a *memory* product — authority: analysis + synthesis
AI recreations generate **"plausible but inaccurate statements attributed to the deceased"** — i.e.,
they **put words in a dead person's mouth.** For a generic chatbot that's a quality issue; for HTN
it is **identity-corrupting**, because HTN's entire promise is *truthful, preserved* memory. A
hallucinating persona converts "this is what they said" into "this is what a model guessed they'd
say" — turning a memory archive into **generated fiction attributed to the dead.** This is not an
edge bug; it **inverts the product's core value.** (Synthesis: the defensible line for a memory
product is **grounded/extractive** — surface only what the person actually recorded/said, with
provenance: "she said this on 12 March 2019" — never **generative impersonation** that invents new
utterances.)

## 4. The legal vacuum — authority: official + analysis
- **No US federal post-mortem privacy protection** (the Privacy Act of 1974 covers the **living**
  only). Mirrors **GDPR Recital 27** (deceased out of scope) from
  [posthumous-delivery-inheritance](posthumous-delivery-inheritance.md). So the deceased's likeness/
  voice has **little statutory protection** — the restraint must be **HTN's own policy**, not law.
- Some US states have **post-mortem right-of-publicity / digital-replica** statutes (e.g., recent
  "digital replica" laws aimed at deepfakes of the dead), but coverage is patchy and consumer-
  product application is unsettled. *(Not verified per-state in this pass — flagged unknown.)*
- Training a persona on a deceased person's data still implicates **living** people's data woven
  through it (co-present family in photos/messages) — so GDPR/child-data duties don't fully vanish.

## Domain lessons (apply to future reviews)
1. **For HTN, "digital ghosts" is a trust-incinerator if done as Model B, and a DEPRIORITIZE in
   timing.** HTN is early MVP; its unproven bet is the continuous-capture habit + trustworthy
   privacy. Shipping post-mortem AI personas now spends the product's entire trust budget on the
   domain's most dangerous feature before the core habit is proven.
2. **The consent-clean adjacent move is high-value and cheap: a pre-mortem "self-authored legacy
   voice."** Let owners *record and curate* answers/messages **while alive** (HereAfter-style),
   stored as **future capsules** on the machinery HTN already needs (see posthumous-delivery ref) —
   with **no generative impersonation.** This captures most of the emotional payload, reinforces the
   legacy core value, and sidesteps the entire deadbot ethics minefield.
3. **If any AI-persona feature is ever built, mutual consent is the gate, not a setting.** Both the
   deceased's pre-mortem opt-in AND the bereaved interactant's opt-in are required; persistence must
   be retireable; access adult-only.
4. **A memory product's AI must be grounded/extractive, never generative-impersonation.** Provenance
   on every surfaced utterance. Fabricating new words in a dead person's voice is NEEDS REDESIGN
   regardless of UX polish (Core Principle 6 — trust depends on transparency about source basis).

## Suggested review heuristics (promote to synthesis/review-heuristics.md if they fire 3+ times)
- **H-F1 — Mutual consent or it's a haunting.** Fires on any post-mortem AI persona / griefbot /
  voice-clone-of-the-deceased feature. Test: pre-mortem consent of the recreated person AND opt-in
  of the interactant, plus a retirement path and adult-only access? Fail → NEEDS REDESIGN.
- **H-F2 — Memory AI is grounded, never generative-impersonation.** Fires when AI speaks *as* or
  *for* a real person (living or dead). Test: does it only surface what the person actually said,
  with provenance — not invent new statements? Fail → NEEDS REDESIGN.
- **H-F3 — Self-authored, pre-mortem, capsule-delivered is the only consent-clean path.** Fires when
  scoping any "AI version of a person." Test: is it owner-authored while alive and delivered via the
  existing legacy/capsule machinery, vs. heir-spun-up from a footprint? Post-mortem-reconstruction
  default → DEPRIORITIZE/NEEDS REDESIGN.

## Remaining unknowns
- Whether HTN intends any AI-persona / griefbot feature at all (none in the current profile).
- Per-state US "digital replica" / post-mortem right-of-publicity coverage, and the **Arabic-market**
  legal/cultural stance on AI recreation of the dead (not covered by this US/UK/EU pass — likely the
  most decision-relevant gap given HTN's Arabic language setting).
- Whether HTN's voice/video corpus is even structured for grounded retrieval (provenance-tagged) vs.
  only suitable for opaque generative training.
