# Memory Specialist Workflow — Domain Reference

The persona HTN is held to. There are two distinct practitioner workflows; HTN's
real competition is not just other apps but **the manual process a careful family
already follows.** Per Core Principle 8, the current workaround is the baseline — and
this file IS the workaround.

## Workflow 1 — Personal archiving / DAM (the "preserve + organize" practice) — authority: analysis
The professional Digital Asset Management loop family historians use:
1. **Capture / digitize** — scan or import photos, video, voice; pull in identifying
   info written on/around the original at capture time.
2. **Rename / label consistently** — a naming pattern chosen for *future findability*
   ("how will I search for this later?").
3. **Tag & caption (metadata)** — date, people, place, story. Metadata is the durable
   value; an untagged archive is nearly unsearchable later.
4. **Organize** — decide what to keep, structure so items are findable for decades.
5. **Archive & back up** — regular backups, multiple media, and **media migration to
   avoid format obsolescence** (matches media-durability.md).
6. **Share / export** — export to shareable formats for family.

**What this means for HTN:** the manual workflow's pain points are HTN's value targets —
*capture friction, metadata effort, findability over time, and backup discipline.* HTN
auto-handles 4–5 (organize/backup), but steps 2–3 (label/tag) are the effort that
families abandon, and step 6 (export) is the durability/portability obligation. The
chronological timeline IS the "organize for findability" step done automatically — that's
HTN's genuine improvement over the manual DAM grind.

## Workflow 2 — Digital legacy planning (the "hand off on death" practice) — authority: analysis
The end-of-life process a family follows to pass things on:
1. **Inventory** — list every asset/account and how to access it.
2. **Decide per asset** — for each, what happens on death (keep / transfer / close).
3. **Designate a digital executor** — a specific person with authority to manage the
   estate. (HTN's "designated family member / heir" maps here.)
4. **Family conversation** — start small: "where are the important things, and what
   stories/messages would you want preserved?" The conversation is the real activation.
5. **Review cycle** — revisit every 3–5 years and after major life events (marriage,
   birth, divorce). Legacy plans go stale.

**What this means for HTN:** HTN's Future Capsules + inheritance are a *productized*
version of steps 2–4. But the manual process has two things HTN often lacks: an explicit
**per-item decision** (not one blanket setting) and a **review cadence**. And step 4
reframes activation — the emotional trigger to preserve is often a *family conversation*
or life event, not an app notification (informs the habit/trigger model in
continuous-capture-habit.md).

## Domain lessons (apply to future reviews → added as H-E heuristics)
1. **The baseline is the manual DAM + legacy-planning workflow, not a rival app.** Name it
   in reviews: does HTN meaningfully beat the careful-family manual process? (Core Principle 8.)
2. **Metadata (date/people/place/story) is the durable value** — the step families abandon.
   HTN features that lower tagging effort (or auto-tag) attack the real pain; features that
   add tagging burden work against the persona.
3. **Findability-over-decades is the win the timeline automates** — protect it at scale
   (ties to QC "timeline integrity / scale performance" J4/J5).
4. **Legacy needs per-item decisions + a review cadence**, not one blanket privacy/inheritance
   setting — the manual process is granular and revisited; HTN should be too.
5. **Activation is often a life event / family conversation, not an app cue** — the strongest
   capture trigger may be relational (a birth, a death, an invite), which HTN can design around.
